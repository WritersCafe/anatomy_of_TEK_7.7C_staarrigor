Example 1 — Clear Mystery Suspense

At the beginning of the story, Laila notices that the old school clock keeps stopping at exactly 3:17 each afternoon.

Her friend tells her it is probably broken, but Laila remembers that the missing art club key was last seen near the clock.

Later, strange things begin happening around the school. A hallway light flickers whenever someone walks past the display case. Then the principal announces that the art competition trophy has disappeared.

Laila begins wondering whether the clock, the key, and the trophy are connected.

At the climax, Laila opens the clock and finds the missing key hidden inside.

Question 1 — Notice the Plot Clue

Which detail BEST foreshadows an important later event?

A. The principal announces the trophy is missing.
B. The hallway light flickers near the display case.
C. The clock keeps stopping at 3:17, and the missing key was last seen near the clock.
D. Laila notices strange things around the school.
↓

Question 2 — Track Suspense

Which moment BEST builds suspense by making the reader wonder what will happen next?

A. Laila’s friend says the clock is probably broken.
B. The clock, missing key, flickering light, and missing trophy all seem connected.
C. The story takes place during an art competition.
D. Laila notices strange things around the school.
↓

Question 3 — Connect Foreshadowing to Later Events

How does the early detail about the clock advance the plot?

A. It creates a mysterious mood around the school.
B. It shows that Laila pays attention to details.
C. It proves the clock is the most important object in the story.
D. It plants a clue that becomes important later when Laila discovers the hidden key.
↓

Question 4 — Analyze How Suspense Advances the Plot

How does suspense help move the story forward?

A. The unanswered questions push Laila to continue investigating until the mystery is resolved.
B. The missing trophy makes the story exciting.
C. The story creates a mysterious mood at the school.
D. Laila already knows where the trophy is hidden.

Example 2 — Subtle Emotional Suspense

Before the championship race, Andre folds his lucky blue wristband into his backpack instead of wearing it.

His coach notices but says nothing.

Andre keeps glancing at the empty space on his wrist while the announcer calls runners to the starting line.

Halfway through the race, Andre begins struggling to keep pace.

Near the final stretch, Andre remembers why he stopped wearing the wristband:

He wanted to prove he could trust himself instead of luck.

Question 5 — Find the Subtle Foreshadowing

Which detail BEST foreshadows Andre’s internal conflict?

A. Andre’s coach notices the backpack.
B. Andre is running in a championship race.
C. Andre hides the wristband but keeps glancing at his empty wrist.
D. Andre hears teammates cheering during the race.
↓

Question 6 — Recognize Subtle Suspense

How does the author create suspense without using a mystery?

A. The race itself feels competitive and exciting.
B. Andre has a lucky wristband.
C. Readers wonder whether Andre will win the race.
D. Readers wonder whether Andre will trust himself when pressure builds.

Cognitive Choreography

Move 1: Spot the Plot Clue

Students must notice details that may matter later instead of treating all details equally.

Move 2: Track Reader Questions

Students must recognize what the author is making the reader wonder about.

Move 3: Connect Clues to Events

Students must connect earlier clues to later events in the plot.

Move 4: Analyze Suspense

Students must explain how uncertainty and tension move the story forward.

Move 5: Explain Plot Advancement

Students must explain how foreshadowing and suspense help move the story toward climax and resolution.

Why Students Can Still Struggle with 7.7C

Students often know a story feels suspenseful or emotional…

…but they cannot yet explain HOW the author constructed those effects to advance the plot.

The rigor is not identifying suspense.

The rigor is explaining HOW suspense advances the plot.

Ready for the Rigor Layer?

Now experience what happens when scaffolds disappear and students must independently coordinate the thinking.

7.7C — Closer to STAAR Rigor

  • Foreshadowing becomes more subtle
  • Suspense becomes emotional instead of dramatic
  • Distractors contain partially true information
  • Students must independently explain how plot elements advance the story

Read the Fiction Passage

Nia checked her phone again while the bus bumped toward the regional robotics competition.

Three unread messages from her father still sat at the top of the screen.

“You’ll do great today.”

“Call me after the competition.”

“Good luck.”

Nia slid the phone into her backpack without replying.

At the convention center, teams hurried between tables carrying laptops, wires, and tool kits.

During the first round, the robot suddenly froze in the obstacle course while the timer continued counting down.

Mateo looked at Nia.

“Can you fix it?”

Nia hesitated.

Usually she preferred working quietly behind the scenes instead of explaining problems publicly.

But the timer kept running.

Nia stepped forward, explained the issue to the judge, and quickly reconnected the loose wire.

STAAR-Style Question

How does suspense help advance the plot?

A. The robotics competition feels exciting and competitive.
B. Readers wonder whether the robot will work again.
C. Readers wonder whether Nia will trust herself and step forward publicly under pressure.
D. Mateo depends on Nia to solve technical problems.
